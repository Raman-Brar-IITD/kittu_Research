Ishan was sitting on the balcony of his room, reflecting on his recent return to Patna to stay with his parents after the tournament. The tournament had been a great success for India and especially for Ishan, who had scored two consecutive centuries and felt very proud of himself. Although he wouldn't say it out loud, the person he credited most for his outstanding performance was Shubman and his better health.

Ishan had not performed well in the last two tournaments, both of which coincided with Shubman being in the hospital. During the third tournament, in a moment of impulsive decision-making, he left everything behind and returned to India. Looking back, he realized that it was a rather crazy decision, and he considered it a miracle that the board didn't penalize him for it.

He had been so troubled by everything that he had stopped thinking clearly— in fact, he had stopped thinking altogether. However, he began to realize that the people around him understood him better than he had assumed. Ishan was extremely grateful to all of them. Ishan had been severely affected by shubman's accident and it was a known fact even if he wanted to deny it.

The tournament had ended a week ago, and he had arrived in Patna the very next day. The team had insisted that he join them in Mumbai, but he wanted some time alone, away from everything that stressed him out. As a result, he had politely declined their offers.

Shubman was not allowed to play in the international match yet, but his presence during the practice sessions and the main tournament was crucial for his career. Ishan was glad that Shubman could still join the team in the dugouts, even though he wasn't part of the squad.

Ishan had also turned down the offer to travel with the team because he didn't want to overthink various issues. Ironically, he found himself doing just that while sitting at home. There was no difference in his situation. Most of the thoughts that troubled him somehow revolved around Shubman. At this point, Ishan felt frustrated with himself.

Ishan found himself frequently thinking about Shubman, even when they were apart, and it was becoming increasingly difficult to ignore. This wasn't the first time he had experienced this; however, the intensity of these thoughts was unsettling. The more Ishan contemplated the situation, the more he wished the earth would swallow him whole.

He had never imagined that he would one day sit and question his own sanity over thoughts of Shubman. Ishan had always kept Shubman in his mind, yet he never considered that best friends might not constantly ponder each other's lives. Perhaps best friends didn't need to know every detail about one another.

Ishan had always believed their interactions were typical of best friends—until Shubman shattered his comfortable delusion by confessing his feelings. That confession rattled Ishan to his core, compelling him to re-evaluate his entire perspective on their relationship. Suddenly, all the small, tender moments they had shared took on a new significance in Ishan's mind.

He wanted to convince himself that it might not be the case at all, that maybe Shubman was just acting out because of his temporary dementia. He hoped that all the moments they shared were as natural as they felt, that their experiences together as friends were truly nothing more than that.

But who was he kidding? Ishan could be as clueless and oblivious as a koala, yet even he couldn't ignore the reality that their bond was far deeper than mere platonic friendship. Ishan had realized this long before today, but he chose to deceive himself into believing otherwise.

Ishan was lost in thought when he heard a soft knock on his door. "Come in," he said absent-mindedly. Pallavi entered the room, holding his phone. "Ishu, you left your phone downstairs after lunch. Shubman's mom called, but the call was declined before I could answer. You should talk to her; it might be something urgent." She handed him the phone, flashed him a smile, and left the room before he could thank her.

Ishan opened his call log, wondering why Shubman's mom had called him. "Hello?" he said as she picked up. For a few seconds, there was no response, just dead silence. Ishan started to worry that there might be a network issue. Just as he contemplated ending the call, he heard an unmistakable sob from the other end.

"Hello, Aunty? What's wrong?" Ishan said, now feeling anxious. He could hear some commotion in the background, and soon he recognized Shahneel's voice. "Hello, Ishan. Don't worry too much; it's just Shubman who fainted, and Mom started calling everyone in a panic."

"SHUBMAN WHAT?" The blood rushed to Ishan's head, and he couldn't think straight for a moment. "Relax, Ishii. It's nothing; he was probably just remembering some forgotten memories. It's okay; we took him to the hospital. He'll be alright," Shahneel tried to calm Ishan down, but he could hear the worry in her tone.

Ishan could see through the situation. There was no way Shubman's mom would call him out of the blue just because she panicked. Shahneel would never sound so concerned over something trivial. It had to be a much bigger issue, and Shahneel was just trying to keep him from worrying. "Okay, I'm on my way," he said, canceling the call before Shahneel could say anything else.

Ishan quickly searched for the earliest plane tickets and grabbed a random bag, filling it with his essentials. About an hour later, he came downstairs, bag in hand. "Where are you going?" Pallavi asked, eyeing the bag. "Shubman has fainted."

The moment he spoke, his family abandoned everything they were doing to ask him what he meant. They all looked just as worried as Ishan. "I don't know much, and Shahneel Di has said that it isn't something to worry about, but I can feel the tension. I have a bad feeling about this."

"Do you want me to book the tickets for you?" Raj asked, picking up his phone from the side table. "It's okay; I already booked the tickets." "Are they in Mumbai?" his mom asked, concern etched on her face. "Yes. I remember Shubman mentioning that they had all moved there for the time being. I'm sure that's where they are right now."

"And which hospital is he admitted to?" his father asked. "It has to be the same one as before since he gets his routine check-ups there after the accident." "When is your flight scheduled?" Pallavi inquired. "In about two hours," he replied. "You should get going then," she said after hearing his answer. "I'll drive you to the airport," Raj offered as he grabbed his keys.

"Ishan," his mom called out just as he was about to get into the car. "Yeah?" he responded. "Don't worry too much. Maybe they're right; perhaps it isn't anything to be overly concerned about. Call us after you arrive. We'll come by later," she reassured him. "Okay." With that, he left home.

While he was still on the flight, Ishan received a phone call from Virat. "Hello, bhai?" "Are you coming to Mumbai?" "I'm on my way, yes. Why?" "Well, you could have come tomorrow; we were here to take care of things for the time being. Don't stress too much, okay? Just get here safely."

Ishan recognized the same tone in Virat's voice as he had heard from his mother earlier. He was not a child to be so careless; he knew how to get there safely. What Ishan hadn't realized was the extent of his worry for Shubman and its implications.

Ishan didn't understand it himself, but everyone had witnessed his reaction when Shubman first had the accident months earlier. The doctors weren't even sure they could save him. Since then, Ishan had made it a routine to stay at the hospital for as long as he could each day.

He had neglected his practice and completely given up on everything unrelated to Shubman. This indifference severely affected his performance, but who could say anything to him about it? Even the public took notice. For the first time in Indian cricket history, a player was not trolled for poor performance, even during such an important series. Ishan looked so exhausted that even his detractors felt sympathy for him at that moment.

Now that he had fainted again, the team was reluctant to go through it all once more. They knew that Ishan was blaming himself for the incident. They had made it clear that it wasn't his fault, but he was stubbornly reluctant to accept that. Ishan hadn't explicitly stated that he felt responsible for the accident, but those around him could see it nonetheless.
