The confession was sweet, and it had left Ishan with a quiet kind of joy—the kind that settles deep in your chest and makes everything feel a little softer, a little lighter. He had been thinking about Shubman, about the confession that he had made since the England tour.

No matter how much he tried to ignore it, the overthinking came back to him every day. The thoughts of losing himself in search of something more had been haunting his dreams for some time now, but he also knew that the pushing back of the thoughts, the deliberate changing of focus, wasn't just because he didn't want to accept his feelings. Ishan was scared. Scared to hurt shubman--- again.

Ishan had known that something was charging between him and Shubman for some time now. He was trying to turn a blind eye to it, was trying to be as oblivious as possible, but there was no denying the fact that he had noticed the subtle touches and longing looks that Shubman threw his way.

Shubman...his shubman. Ishan knew him the best. Ishan would know if he was feeling uncomfortable even in the slightest without Shubman having to explicitly say it in words. Between him and Shubman, words had been overrated anyway. Shubman could take 0.02 seconds more to answer a question, and Ishan would know that there was something wrong.

Of course, he knew when Shubman started to feel something more than friendship towards him. At first, it had been a fleeting doubt, one that would often arise during an interview or maybe a net practice. A doubt that gave way to no further thinking or any kind of discomfort.

Then Ishan started to notice, along with the world, the prolonged stares and the touches that lasted longer than expected. The way Shubman's eyes found him in the crowd didn't feel the same as before; there was something different about the way Shubman reacted to his touches now.

Ishan had known; he had always known. It was his shubman after all. There was something desperate with the way Ishan went out of his way to avoid Shubman in the last few weeks before Shubman's accident. Don't get him wrong; he wasn't disgusted by Shubman or his feelings. No, Ishan was not disgusted or repelled. He was scared.

Ishan was scared of his own heart sometimes. He was scared that he would unknowingly hurt Shubman. He had thoughts, thoughts that would take up his entire nights and the majority of his consciousness and still wouldn't die down. Thoughts of harming their friendship in any way felt like torture beyond words for Ishan.

He had always thanked the heavens for Shubman. In the world of this competitive sport, he had not only gotten himself a close friend, a partner in crime, a loyal mate, or a personal cheerleader; no, Ishan had gotten himself home.

The late-night gossip, the ice cream parlor trips, the nightly movie marathons, and the religious phone calls had all hit home for Ishan, and he wouldn't trade them for anything. Not even the idea of having Shubman as something more could convince Ishan to risk a potential chance of ruining what they already had.

So no, Ishan wasn't disgusted; he was scared. Scared of ruining their friendship, scared of risking Shubman. Ishan was not a gambler, and if there was one thing that he would never gamble, it was Shubman. Ishan would choose death over having to lose Shubman. He had been so resolute in this resolve that he had overlooked the possibility of hurting Shubman unintentionally.

He had played dumb to all the love stares from Shubman, 'He had always looked at me like that; there is nothing different.' He told himself every time he caught Shubman staring. He had turned a deaf ear to all the teasings that Shubman got from their teammates. He had made fun of all the edited videos of him and Shubman online. Had laughed at the audacity of people to ship him with Shubman.

He blamed it on the weather when he felt hot after walking in on Shubman, who had just gotten out of a bath. 'It isn't like I haven't seen him half-naked before. Fuck the global warming.' He muttered under his breath after clumsily slamming the door shut. 'I have a heart issue; I am sure of it,' he thought after his heart beat a little too fast after that one eye contact with Shubman on the field.

During the last few weeks before the accident, Ishan was so immersed in avoiding his feelings and Shubman's feelings altogether that he hadn't noticed Shubman hurting just a bit more every time Ishan had refused an ice cream date, or a movie invitation, or something as simple as sitting with him at the dinner.

Ishan was so scared to hurt Shubman that he was hurting him trying to avoid the same. It had all led to that one fateful fight they had just before Shubman got into that accident. "Shubman, can you pass this list to Rohit Bhai?" Ishan said without looking up from his laptop. That is what he mostly did: kept himself busy and away from Shubman and his thoughts.

"Can you at least look at me when you ask me for something, or do you hate me so much that you can't even bear to look at my face?" Shubman said, his voice cracking just a bit at the end. That slip had made Ishan look at him with confusion etched on his face. "What?" "Do you hate me now?"

Shubman's resolve was at its end. He had waited for Ishan to come out of whatever he had been facing. To come to Shubman like he always did, but this has been too harsh for Shubman's heart. The more he tried waiting, the more it hurt him.  In the end, Shubman decided to confront Ishan.

"Why would I hate you, Shubman?" The use of his full name didn't help Shubman's breaking heart. He took the paper from Ishan's grip and went away to do what he was asked. "Shubman. Shubman, look at me." Ishan called behind him, but Shubman was adamant about not showing him how weak he was for him.

The air was thick with something more than just summer heat — it was the weight of too many things left unsaid. Shubman stormed down the steps of the stadium, jaw clenched, vision swimming, heart in his throat.

"Shubman, wait—!" Ishan called out, voice cracking as he stumbled after him. But Shubman didn't stop. He couldn't. Not after the way Ishan had been treating him lately — like a stranger. Like he didn't matter. Like none of what they'd shared ever happened.

His nails dug into his palms as he walked faster, trying to outrun the lump rising in his throat. The blur in his eyes had nothing to do with the humidity. "I'm talking to you, dammit!" Ishan shouted behind him, feet pounding against the pavement. Shubman didn't turn around.

"You don't get to act like I'm the problem when you're the one who's been shutting me out!" Ishan's breath hitched. "I was trying to protect you—!" "By hurting me?!" That was the first time Shubman's voice cracked, and it made Ishan freeze for a second. It was trembling and loud and thick with emotion.

Shubman didn't stop walking. "You think you're protecting me? By pretending I don't matter anymore? By looking through me like I'm nothing? God, Ishan. You're not protecting me. You're just... you're just breaking me." "Shubman, please," Ishan tried, picking up pace again. "I didn't mean to— I thought if I stayed away, it'd be easier—"

"For who?" Shubman shouted, whirling around now as he crossed the road, walking backward to face him. "Because it wasn't easier for me, Ishan! Not even for a second!" Ishan finally stepped into the road, distracted, focused only on him.

And in that awful second, Shubman's smile dropped. His pupils dilated. His voice came out in a panic-stricken scream. "ISHAN—!" Ishan barely had time to process it — the blur of headlights, the screech of brakes, the roar of a horn. Then shove. Cold wind. Screaming metal. Skin meeting pavement.

And silence. Time froze for a second.

Ishan blinked, dazed, looking around, trying to understand why the world tilted on its axis. Then his gaze dropped—and his heart left his body. "Shubman?" he whispered. Shubman was lying just a few feet away, motionless.

"SHUBMAN—"

Ishan shouted himself awake, yanked out of the memory that had been tormenting him for months. His hand was being held tightly—someone was there—but everything else was chaos. Voices swarmed around him, but he couldn't make out a single face. There was only darkness in front of him, thick and absolute. His eyes refused to open. Maybe he didn't want them to. Maybe he couldn't bear the thought of seeing Shubman lying there—still, fragile—all because he'd chosen to be an idiot.

There was bile rising in his throat. He wanted to cry, but even his tears had betrayed him. Breathing felt impossible. It was like the oxygen had been punched out of his system like grief had made a home in his lungs. He tried reaching out to the fleeting figures in the dark, desperate to find something to anchor him.

"Ishan, inhale." He followed the voice like it was gospel. He inhaled. "Exhale." He exhaled. "Inhale." He obeyed. "Exhale." With every breath, the oxygen slowly began to kick in—making him dizzy but conscious. "Look at me. Look at the torch."

There was a faint light ahead, a soft beam slicing through the blackness. Ishan tried to focus on it. His eyes strained, struggling to adjust until a face finally began to come into view.

The light grew brighter than expected, and he instinctively squinted. A bottle was pressed into his hands. He took a sip, his throat dry and scratchy, and looked up again. Concern was etched into every line of the person's face. Then came a light slap on his cheek, more grounding than alarming.

Just as he opened his mouth to react, a sharp pain flared up in his arm. "Ah—!" "You okay?" "What the fuck was that?" he rasped, breathless from the jolt. Before anyone could answer—before he could get a sense of who was around him—he was pulled into a hug.

Not just any hug. A hug that felt like home. He melted into it instantly, eyes stinging, throat closing. He clung to Shubman like he'd fall apart if he let go. And maybe he would've.

His innocent, sweet Shubman. His Shubman, who had fallen in love with him again even after forgetting who he was. His Shubman, who never once blamed him, not even after the memories returned. His Shubman, who, instead of yelling or asking for answers, was holding him like he was the one who almost lost everything. His Shubman, who had every right to be furious, to shout, to shove—but chose to stay soft.

His Shubman.

The tears weren't part of the plan. But they came anyway, hot and relentless. To be honest, the Shubman part of Ishan's life had never really followed any plan. He had the vague awareness that people were around—some concerned, others teasing—but none of them mattered. Not right now.

Nothing mattered when he was being held so close. When Shubman's heartbeat thumped steady and warm against his ear. When his presence alone was enough to calm the quake inside him. When the moment—the one that had shattered Ishan to the core—had finally passed. When Shubman was alive, safe, and right here.

That truth alone lulled Ishan toward sleep. A kind of sleep he hadn't known in months. The kind without worry. Without pain. In the arms of the person who meant more to him than breath itself.

This hadn't been on Ishan's to-do list. Not because he didn't want it—he just never thought he was lucky enough to deserve it. But sometimes life gives you more than you think you're worth. And maybe that's the quiet, aching beauty of wanting.

Of yearning.

Of finally having.
